# SEO Content Strategy

A skill for creating and optimizing content that ranks in Google, based directly on Google's official guidelines for helpful, people-first content and the E-E-A-T framework.

## The Fundamental Principle

Google's core ranking systems reward content created **primarily to help people**, not content made to manipulate search rankings. Every content decision should start from the question: *"Does this genuinely help a real person who landed on this page?"*

This is not a philosophical preference — it's how Google's systems are designed to work. Content optimized for humans tends to rank; content optimized for search engines tends to get filtered or penalized.

---

## E-E-A-T: The Quality Framework

Google evaluates content through the lens of **Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness**. Of these, **Trust is most important** — the others contribute to it.

| Signal | What It Means | How to Demonstrate It |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| **Experience** | First-hand experience with the topic | "When I visited..." / actual photos / specific details only an eyewitness would know |
| **Expertise** | Knowledge depth in the field | Accurate, specific information / credentials mentioned / nuanced perspective |
| **Authoritativeness** | Recognition as a source worth trusting | Links from reputable sites / author with established background / cited by others |
| **Trustworthiness** | Honest, accurate, transparent | Sources cited / factual errors absent / disclosures made / author bio present |

**YMYL Note:** Topics affecting money, health, or safety get extra scrutiny. Travel fits this — people are spending significant money and safety is a concern. Content must be especially reliable.

## The "Who, How, Why" Test

Before writing any piece of content, answer these three questions:

**Who created this?**
- Is the author identifiable?
- Is there a byline? Does it link to an author bio?
- Does the bio show relevant experience (e.g., "has led 200+ tours to Egypt")?

**How was it created?**
- Was it based on first-hand experience?
- Does it show the research/testing process?
- If AI-assisted, is that disclosed appropriately?

**Why does it exist?**
- Is it primarily to help the reader?
- Or primarily to rank and get traffic?

If the "why" is traffic-first, reframe the content until it's genuinely useful first.

## Search Intent: Match What the Searcher Actually Wants

Before writing, identify the **search intent** of the target keyword. There are four types:

| Intent Type | What the Searcher Wants | Example Query | Content Format |
|-------------|------------------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Informational** | Learn something | "is Egypt safe for tourists" | In-depth guide, FAQ |
| **Navigational** | Find a specific site | "Access Egypt Travel reviews" | Brand page, homepage |
| **Commercial** | Research before buying | "best Egypt tour companies" | Comparison, review, "why us" |
| **Transactional** | Ready to buy | "book Egypt tour package" | Tour page with pricing + CTA |

**Matching intent matters more than keyword density.** If someone searches "is Egypt safe" they want reassurance and facts — not a tour booking page. Give them what matches why they searched.

## Keyword Research Framework

### Start with your audience, not tools
What questions do your customers ask before booking? In emails, calls, social DMs? These are your keywords.

### Keyword targeting tiers

**Tier 1 — High-value, competitive (long-term goal)**
Generic, high-volume terms. Hard to rank for as a small site.
- "egypt tours", "egypt vacation packages", "nile cruise"
- Strategy: Can compete if content is meaningfully better than big operators

**Tier 2 — Specific, moderate competition (medium-term)**
More specific queries where genuine expertise wins.
- "egypt tours for first timers", "egypt itinerary 10 days", "nile cruise from luxor"
- Strategy: These are your bread and butter — write the best, most specific answer

**Tier 3 — Long-tail, low competition (start here)**
Very specific queries, low search volume, but high conversion intent.
- "is egypt safe for american solo female traveler 2025"
- "egypt tour for families with young kids"
- "egypt pyramid tour without large groups"
- Strategy: Win these first — they convert better and build domain authority

### Keyword placement (natural, not stuffed)
- Primary keyword in: title, H1, first 100 words, at least one H2, URL, meta description
- Secondary/related keywords: naturally throughout the content
- Never: stuff keywords awkwardly, repeat exact phrases robotically

## Content Quality Standards

### What makes content rank (Google's self-assessment questions)

Content should answer YES to all of these:
- Does it provide **original** information, reporting, or analysis not found elsewhere?
- Does it provide a **comprehensive** description of the topic?
- Does it provide **insightful** analysis beyond the obvious?
- Is the heading/title **accurately descriptive** (not clickbait)?
- Is this something you'd **bookmark or share**?
- Would you expect to see this **in a reputable publication**?
- Does it provide **substantial value** vs. competing pages in search results?

### Red flags that hurt rankings

Content that answers YES to these is at risk:
- Made primarily to attract search traffic (not to help people)
- Summarizes other sources without adding value
- Written to hit a word count (there is no correct word count — write as much as needed)
- Targets trending topics unrelated to your business expertise
- Leaves readers needing to search again for better answers
- Mass-produced across many topics without genuine expertise
- Dates changed to appear fresh without substantive content updates

## Content Structure Blueprint

### Title (H1)
- Specific, descriptive, accurate
- Includes primary keyword naturally
- Not clickbait, not keyword-stuffed
- Example: "Is Egypt Safe for American Tourists? What the State Department Doesn't Tell You"

### Introduction (First 100 words)
- Answer the question or make the value proposition immediately
- Don't bury the lead
- Hook: specific, surprising, or personally relevant to the reader
- Establish credibility quickly (first-hand experience signal)

### Body: Structure for Skimmers and Readers
- Use H2s and H3s to break up sections — they help both readers and Google
- Short paragraphs (3–4 lines max for web)
- Bullet points and tables for comparative or list information
- Bold key phrases (don't bold everything — dilutes impact)
- Internal links to related pages (helps Google understand site structure + keeps visitors)

### Practical Answer / Specific Details
- The section Google is most likely to use for a featured snippet
- Use a clear question as an H2, then a direct, concise answer immediately below
- Follow with supporting detail
- Example:
  ```
  ## Is Egypt Safe for American Tourists in 2025?
  Yes. Egypt ranks as Level 2 (Exercise Normal Precautions) by the US State Department — the same level as France, Germany, and Japan. Tourist sites have heavy security presence...
  ```

### Differentiated Perspective
- What can you say that competitors can't?
- First-hand stories, specific guide names, specific locations, real photos
- Concrete details > vague generalities

### Conclusion + CTA
- Summarize the key takeaway
- Clear next action: contact us, book a tour, read a related post
- Don't end abruptly

## On-Page SEO Execution

After content is written, run this checklist:

- [ ] Title tag: unique, ~55 chars, includes primary keyword
- [ ] Meta description: unique, ~155 chars, written to earn the click
- [ ] URL: short, lowercase, hyphens, includes keyword (`/is-egypt-safe-for-tourists/`)
- [ ] H1: matches or closely relates to title
- [ ] Primary keyword in first 100 words
- [ ] At least one H2 includes a related keyword
- [ ] Images: descriptive alt text, compressed, descriptive filenames
- [ ] Internal links: 2–3 links to related pages on your site
- [ ] Author byline present with bio link
- [ ] Publication date visible
- [ ] Schema markup added (Article for blog, FAQ if applicable, BreadcrumbList)
- [ ] Content answers the search intent fully — would a visitor need to search again?

---

## Content Freshness

Google has "query deserves freshness" systems for time-sensitive topics.

- **Update** existing posts when facts change (safety updates, pricing, regulations)
- **Change the date only when content substantially changes** — Google can detect date manipulation
- Add a "Last updated: [date]" note when meaningful updates are made
- Don't create thin new posts on the same topic — update the existing one instead

## Optimizing for AI Overviews and Generative AI Search

Google's AI Overviews (and AI Mode) use the same ranking systems as regular search — the difference is how results are presented. Google uses **retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)**: it pulls relevant pages from its index, then uses AI to synthesize an answer and cite sources. Being cited in an AI Overview requires being indexed and ranking for the query first.

**What this means in practice:**
- Regular SEO best practices remain the foundation — there is no separate "AI SEO"
- Pages must be indexed and eligible to appear with snippets (technical requirements apply)
- Crawlability matters: AI systems use publicly accessible, crawlable content

### Non-Commodity vs. Commodity Content

This is the most important concept for AI search visibility. Google's AI actively filters for sources that add unique value.

**Commodity content** (likely to be skipped by AI Overviews):
- "7 Tips for First-Time Homebuyers" — common knowledge anyone could write
- Summaries of what other sites already said
- Content that could easily be produced by an AI model itself

**Non-commodity content** (what AI Overviews cite):
- First-hand reviews with specific, personal observations only an eyewitness would know
- Data, research, or analysis not available elsewhere
- Genuine local expertise and insider knowledge
- Original perspective that goes beyond common knowledge

**Test:** Ask "Could an AI model write this without having experienced it?" If yes, it's commodity.

### AI Optimization Myths — What Does NOT Work

Google has explicitly debunked these:

| Claim | Reality |
|-------|---------|
| "Create llms.txt files" | Google Search ignores them — they help other AI systems, not Google |
| "Chunk content into small pieces" | Google understands nuance across full pages — no chunking needed |
| "Write for every keyword variation" | AI understands synonyms and intent — don't create thin pages for every variation |
| "Seek inauthentic mentions" | Spam detection catches this — organic mentions from quality content are what counts |
| "Add special AI schema markup" | No special schema exists for AI Overviews |
| "Rewrite content for AI" | Write for humans — AI reads it the same way |

### Content Signals That Help AI Citations

1. **Unique point of view** — first-hand experience, personal observations, original research
2. **Clear structure** — paragraphs, headings, easy navigation
3. **High-quality images and video** — AI Overviews pull in visuals; follow image SEO best practices
4. **Page experience** — fast loading, mobile-friendly, clear main content
5. **No duplicate content** — AI systems skip redundant pages

## Google Discover Optimization

Google Discover is an interest-based feed (not keyword-driven search) that shows content based on user interests and past activity. It appears on mobile Google homepage and in the Google app. Discover traffic is **supplemental and volatile** — treat it as a bonus, not a primary traffic source.

**Eligibility:** Any indexed page meeting Discover content policies. No special markup required.

### Image Requirements for Discover (Critical)

Discover is highly visual. Large images dramatically increase Discover visibility.

- **Minimum width:** 1200 px
- **Minimum resolution:** 300,000+ total pixels (e.g., 1280×720 = 921,600 px ✓)
- **Aspect ratio:** 16:9 preferred
- **Required meta tag:** `<meta name="robots" content="max-image-preview:large">` — without this, Google won't show large preview in Discover
- **Specify preferred image via og:image:**
  ```html
  <meta property="og:image" content="https://yoursite.com/images/egypt-pyramids.jpg">
  ```
- Avoid generic images (logos, text-heavy images) as og:image

### Content Rules for Discover

- **No clickbait** — titles must accurately represent the content
- **No sensationalism** — no outrage bait, morbid curiosity, titillation tactics
- **No information withholding** — don't hide crucial context needed to understand the article
- **Timely OR evergreen** — trending topics and deep interest content both work
- **Page experience matters** — fast load, minimal intrusive interstitials

### Monitoring Discover Traffic

In Search Console: **Performance → Discover tab** — shows impressions, clicks, CTR for last 16 months. Discover traffic naturally fluctuates — don't chase individual spikes.

## Featured Snippets

Featured snippets ("position zero") appear above regular results. Google selects them automatically — **you cannot mark a page as a featured snippet**.

**How to write for featured snippet eligibility:**
1. Use an H2 or H3 as a direct question: "Is Egypt Safe for American Tourists?"
2. Provide a concise, direct answer in the first 1–2 sentences immediately below
3. Follow with supporting detail, lists, or tables
4. Target length: ~40–60 words for the answer section

**To opt out of featured snippets:**
- Add `<meta name="robots" content="nosnippet">` to block all snippets
- Or use `max-snippet: [N]` to limit length (shorter = less likely to be featured)
